## The Problem

Most decisions in organizations, open-source communities, and collaborative projects are made by the people who talk the most, not the people who know the most. A standup where one person dominates the conversation, a GitHub issue where three vocal contributors drive the direction while fifty silent stakeholders have opinions they never express, a team retro where the same five people always speak — these aren't communication problems, they're structural problems. The medium itself (a meeting, a thread, a document) privileges extroversion, seniority, and confidence over expertise, insight, and genuine dissent.

I experienced this firsthand running a university hackathon with 200+ participants and 40 judges. The post-event retrospective was dominated by the organizing team's loudest members, and it took three anonymous surveys and two one-on-one conversations to surface the actual issues — the ones nobody felt comfortable raising in a group setting. That gap — between what the group says it thinks and what individuals actually believe — is the problem PulseVote exists to solve.

## What It Does

PulseVote replaces the "let's go around the room" decision pattern with structured, anonymous, real-time polling that surfaces the full distribution of opinions, not just the majority. A facilitator creates a session (a team meeting, a community vote, a design review) and adds decision items — each one a clear, specific question with optional context, evidence, and discussion threads attached.

Participants join via a link — no account required, no app install, works in any browser on any device. They see each decision one at a time, respond anonymously (or pseudonymously, if the facilitator chooses), and see the aggregated results update in real time. The key innovation is the response model: instead of a simple up/down vote, each decision supports multiple axes — "Strongly Agree" through "Strongly Disagree" with a confidence slider alongside it. This captures not just WHAT someone thinks, but HOW SURE they are, which is often the more informative signal.

After voting, participants can see the full distribution: not just the average, but the shape (consensus, polarized, split, indifferent), the confidence distribution, and — crucially — whether the people who disagreed are confident in their disagreement or uncertain. A polarized result where both sides are confident is a very different signal from a result where the majority is ambivalent and a confident minority disagrees, and PulseVote renders those two scenarios differently.

For recurring sessions (weekly team syncs, sprint retrospectives), PulseVote tracks sentiment over time — not as a gamified metric, but as a genuine trend signal. If the team's confidence in the sprint plan has been declining for three weeks, that's a pattern worth discussing before it becomes a crisis.

## How It's Built

PulseVote is a real-time WebSocket application built on a FastAPI backend with a Next.js frontend, connected through Redis Pub/Sub for live result broadcasting.

The **Session Service** (FastAPI, Python) manages session lifecycle: creation, participant join/leave, decision item management, and result aggregation. Each session is a lightweight state machine — Draft → Open → Voting → Closed → Archived. The service exposes a REST API for CRUD operations and a WebSocket endpoint for real-time voting. Sessions are ephemeral by default (data retained for 30 days), with an optional archive mode for long-running organizational decisions.

The **Voting Engine** (Python, in-process) handles the core aggregation logic. When a vote is cast, it updates the in-memory distribution for that decision item and publishes the new distribution to Redis. The aggregation is purely mathematical — no ML, no weighting — because the goal is transparency, not interpretation. The shape classification (consensus, polarized, etc.) uses a simple heuristic based on distribution spread and confidence variance, documented explicitly so every participant can verify that the classification matches their intuition.

The **Real-time Broadcast Layer** (Redis Pub/Sub + WebSocket) pushes result updates to connected clients as votes come in. The latency target is under 100ms from vote submission to result update on all connected clients. Redis Pub/Sub handles the fan-out; each FastAPI WebSocket connection subscribes to the relevant session channel and receives updates without polling.

The **Frontend** (Next.js, Tailwind) is deliberately lightweight — there are no charts, no graphs, no data visualization library. The results distribution is rendered as CSS-only horizontal bars with shape labels, because the moment you add a chart library, you're making implicit design choices about what the data should look like, and the whole point of PulseVote is surfacing the data without editorializing it. Accessibility is first-class: all voting interactions are keyboard-navigable, screen-reader-friendly, and the result distributions are described in text as well as visually.

The **Persistence Layer** (PostgreSQL) stores sessions, decisions, votes, and trend data. The schema is designed for time-series-friendly queries — session results are stored as normalized rows, not JSON blobs, so trend queries (how did confidence in sprint planning change over the last quarter?) are fast without requiring a separate analytics database.

## Decisions & Tradeoffs

1. Chose anonymous-by-default over optional anonymity because optional anonymity is effectively no anonymity — in practice, the people who opt in are the same people who would have spoken up anyway, and the silent majority stays silent. Making anonymity the default shifts the entire participation dynamic: the signal you get is closer to what people actually think, not what they're willing to say with their name attached.

2. Decided NOT to build an AI summary feature. Every design document and product review I've read proposes "AI-generated meeting summaries" as a natural companion to collaborative tools. I think that's wrong for this use case. The whole point of PulseVote is that the output is simple, transparent, and verifiable: you can count the votes yourself and confirm the distribution. An AI summary introduces a black box between the participants and the result, undermining the trust the tool is designed to build. If PulseVote is successful, someone else can build an AI layer on top of its API.

3. Chose CSS-only result visualization instead of a chart library despite the implementation cost. Chart libraries (Chart.js, D3, Vega) produce prettier visualizations, but they also introduce accessibility barriers (canvas-based rendering, complex SVG structures that screen readers can't parse) and an implicit editorial layer — every chart type encodes assumptions about what's important. CSS horizontal bars with text labels are less visually impressive but more honest, more accessible, and faster to render.

4. The trend-over-time feature is the one I'm least confident about, not because the implementation is hard (it's a straightforward time-series query) but because I'm not sure how teams will actually use it. The risk is that trend visualization becomes a toxic metric — "our confidence score dropped this week, someone must be failing" — rather than a diagnostic signal. I'm keeping the feature but deliberately NOT surfacing it in the default view; it's one click away, not in the dashboard. If teams find it useful, they'll find it.

## What's Next

The core voting flow is implemented and working: session creation, anonymous voting with confidence sliders, real-time result aggregation, and the CSS distribution view. The immediate next step is end-to-end testing with a real group (the hackathon organizing team that inspired the project) to validate that the anonymity model and result visualization actually surface different signals than a normal discussion would.

After the validation sprint, the roadmap is: (1) recurring session support with trend tracking, (2) a lightweight facilitator dashboard with session templates, (3) optional pseudonymous participation (useful for larger communities where participants need persistent identities across sessions), and (4) team plan and pricing — PulseVote will be free for small teams (up to 10 participants, unlimited sessions) with a paid tier for larger organizations that need session archiving and trend analytics.
